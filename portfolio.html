Charting my path

Growing up surrounded by the Pacific Northwest’s mountains and forests inspired my childhood aspiration of becoming a “naturalist explorer.” This was a job that my parents made up to appease my desire to draw maps and see remarkable animals for a living. While I don’t think this job actually exists, I have held onto this exploratory mindset at UW by engaging with various academic subjects, research opportunities, and communities over my time here. In this portfolio, I map out how these layered experiences have shaped me into the scientist-student-leader that I am today.

Academics

I came to the UW from my hometown of Olympia, WA knowing little about what I wanted to do, so I took classes from physics to literature to philosophy during my freshman year to narrow down my interests. I found that I fell in love with chemistry through the Honors General Chemistry series. In retrospect, these classes laid the foundation for my choice of a Biochemistry major.

My social science and humanities classes also had a hand in shaping how I think about a career in science. I still vividly remember an Interdisciplinary Honors class on health inequities that forced me to reckon with how science, despite people’s (often) best intentions, can have detrimental effects on society. These realizations helped shape my desire to promote a more equitable and just scientific community.

Mapping what's next

Now, three and a half years after this journey began, I am graduating this spring with Interdisciplinary and Departmental Honors in Biochemistry and minors in Data Science and Chemistry. Through my academic, research, and community-building experiences at the UW, I have discovered that I want to pursue a career in science. Specifically, I aspire to be a professor leading a research team investigating the molecular mechanisms of the immune system. I then hope to harness this knowledge to design therapeutics for globally impactful infectious diseases and underresearched autoimmune diseases.

To reach this goal, I am thrilled to be heading to the University of Cambridge next year to obtain a master’s in Pathology after being one of 16 Americans this year awarded a Churchill Scholarship. I will be learning immunological techniques that will build on my UW research experiences and set me up to pursue my Ph.D. in Biophysics at UC Berkeley starting the following year. I anticipate that working in an international context will help me grow as a person and scientist and create international collaborations during my career, which are necessary to confront biomedical issues like infectious diseases with worldwide health impacts.
