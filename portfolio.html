Charting my path

Growing up surrounded by the Pacific Northwest’s mountains and forests inspired my childhood aspiration of becoming a “naturalist explorer.” This was a job that my parents made up to appease my desire to draw maps and see remarkable animals for a living. While I don’t think this job actually exists, I have held onto this exploratory mindset at UW by engaging with various academic subjects, research opportunities, and communities over my time at UW. In my Honors portfolio, I map out how these layered experiences have shaped me into the scientist-in-progress that I am today.

Although as a Biochemistry major, I’ve taken more science classes than anything else, my social science and humanities classes through the Interdisciplinary Honors program also had a hand in shaping how I think about a career in science, both philosophically and practically. The realizations stemming from these classes helped shape my desire to promote a more equitable and just scientific community and prompted my involvement in science outreach work at the UW. These academic explorations have also led to explorations into new research areas, which I will highlight in this portfolio, and have helped me chart my path forward after graduating from UW.

Before I begin mapping out this journey, it’s important for me to note that I started my college experience in the fall of 2020 with fully online classes during the peak of the Covid-19 pandemic. In recognition of the challenges that such a start entailed, I pay homage to that time by centering the spaces around campus that defined my time because, looking back, this spatial awareness was essential to my college experience. In a world where so much was and still is virtual, it is easy to forget the real groundedness of the places we find ourselves living. I invite you to read through my portfolio on this page and engage with any of the artifacts that interest you to see how my four years of college have shaped who I am now and who I hope to be in the future.

Academics

I came to the UW from my hometown of Olympia, WA knowing little about what I wanted to do, so I took classes from physics to literature to philosophy during my freshman year to narrow down my interests. I found that I fell in love with chemistry through the Honors General Chemistry series. In retrospect, these classes laid the foundation for my choice of a Biochemistry major.

My social science and humanities classes also had a hand in shaping how I think about a career in science. I still vividly remember an Interdisciplinary Honors class on health inequities that forced me to reckon with how science, despite people’s (often) best intentions, can have detrimental effects on society. These realizations helped shape my desire to promote a more equitable and just scientific community.

Mapping what's next

Now, three and a half years after this journey began, I am graduating this spring with Interdisciplinary and Departmental Honors in Biochemistry and minors in Data Science and Chemistry. Through my academic, research, and community-building experiences at the UW, I have discovered that I want to pursue a career in science. Specifically, I aspire to be a professor leading a research team investigating the molecular mechanisms of the immune system. I then hope to harness this knowledge to design therapeutics for globally impactful infectious diseases and underresearched autoimmune diseases.

To reach this goal, I am thrilled to be heading to the University of Cambridge next year to obtain a master’s in Pathology after being one of 16 Americans this year awarded a Churchill Scholarship. I will be learning immunological techniques that will build on my UW research experiences and set me up to pursue my Ph.D. in Biophysics at UC Berkeley starting the following year. I anticipate that working in an international context will help me grow as a person and scientist and create international collaborations during my career, which are necessary to confront biomedical issues like infectious diseases with worldwide health impacts.
