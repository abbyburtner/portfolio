Bagley Hall: Honors General Chemistry

Based on the fact that I was decent at chemistry in high school and needed honors credits to graduate, I decided to take the Honors General Chemistry series at the UW during my freshman year. This was one of the most pivotal decisions of my college career--and there was little thought put into it! These classes remain some of the most challenging of my college career. I felt clueless a lot of the time, especially in comparison to my classmates who seemed to know everything already from their AP/IB high school chemistry classes. Nevertheless, I just kept my head down and tried to focus on learning the material to catch up. I religiously read almost every page of the textbook, Oxtoby, to start to understand what was going on and, looking back, this experience encapsulates a lot of the way I approach learning and life: I’ll be in the dark for 90% of the time but I can’t let that deter me because eventually it will click and that other 10% will be transformative (probably also why I love running and hiking…a lot of slog for a big payoff). And learning chemistry, really learning it, in a supportive, close-knit environment provided by the Honors series was transformative. I was transfixed by life at the scale of the molecules and atoms and my professors both terrified and inspired me.

Despite the Zoom burnout after three quarters of online college, by the spring quarter class, I had pieced together enough about how academia works to realize that I too could fit into this exciting world of science (the same exciting world that made Covid-19 vaccines widely available that spring of 2021!). That was the first time I considered pursuing a career in science, inspired simultaneously by my first female professor of my college science career and my first research experience. Looking back, these classes were definitely both the threshold of my science career and a transformational moment in my life when I suddenly realized that scientists are just people and I am a person who can be a scientist.

My Art in Science artwork features female X-ray crystallographers and their scientific "superpower" in a twist on Superman's X-ray vision. My idea for this came right after we learned about X-ray diffraction in my chemistry class and about gender's relationship with science in my philosophy class. At the same time, I was reading James Watson's The Double Helix: A Personal Account of the Discovery of the Structure of DNA and I was really shocked about his treatment of Rosalind Franklin (I'd heard the story before, but reading it firsthand is something else...). I had a lot of fun researching these cool women scientists and making this little drawing this quarter.
