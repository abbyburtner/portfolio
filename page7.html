Parrington Hall: Philosophy of AI (CSE 490/HONORS 222)

I vividly remember learning about the famous philosopher of science Thomas Kuhn’s theory of scientific revolutions for the first time in a History and Philosophy of Science class during my freshman year. I was fascinated by the idea that the scientific process doesn't produce objective truth--instead, it produces truths that agree with the paradigm it operates under. And if scientific observations that disagree with that paradigm begin to pile up, that opens the door for a paradigm shift and "scientific revolution". Learning a little bit about the philosophy of science in that class makes me always think twice about how and why we know what we think we know. When I had the opportunity to take a similar class, Philosophy of AI, during spring of my junior year, I jumped at the chance to think more about these fundamental problems in the context of a AI--which was also great timing since ChatGPT had just been released and was starting to shake up the academy.

The class covered everything from the philosophy of morality and consciousness to AI hardware and software aspects. On the surface, it didn’t have much to do with my field of study (Biochemistry), but in the spirit of interdisciplinary thinking, I connected it. For my final project I used a Kuhnian lens to argue that AI is currently used for normal scientific discoveries but could conceivably lead to theoretical scientific discoveries by uncovering anomalies (graphical version of my thesis statement below). I evaluated these claims in the context of a particular scientific area, the protein folding problem in biochemistry (and AlphaFold2's contributions), but these arguments could conceivably apply to many fields of contemporary science. Since I currently conduct research at the interface of experimental and computational biochemistry, this final project epitomizes what interdisciplinary means for me currently and how I envision it playing a role in my future.
