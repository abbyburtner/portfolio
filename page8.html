The Burke Museum of Natural History and Culture: Evolutionary biology research

As alluded to in my introduction, I've always loved to be out in nature and looking at cool animals. This wonder about the natural world is what first drew me to research and inspired me to join the Santana Lab, an evolutionary biology lab in the UW Biology department, early in my first year of undergrad. In her lab, I wanted to understand how bat limbs evolved to be so different from other mammals. The origin of bat flight is still not fully understood due to an uninformative fossil record, but it is widely hypothesized that bats evolved from gliding ancestors. During my sophomore year, I developed an independent research project to test the gliding-to-flying hypothesis of the origin of bat flight. I used computational methods to model the evolution of limb traits on a dataset spanning over 200 mammals. After running hundreds of statistical models covering different evolutionary scenarios, I found that glider forelimb but not hindlimb skeletal traits may fall on an intermediate adaptive peak between ground-dwelling mammals and bats, supporting the gliding-to-flying hypothesis. Based on these results, I wrote a manuscript for publication that is currently in revision in the journal PeerJ.

During this time, I also worked with three undergraduate students on several other collaborative projects examining the vertebral column and appendicular skeletons of mammals, particularly the squirrel family Sciuridae. Despite various technical challenges from data handling and computational limitations as I worked on these projects, I found myself more excited to spend time conducting research than on anything else. By the end of my sophomore year, it became clear that I wanted to pursue a career in research.

I want to emphasize that these projects were key to teaching me the importance of resilience and determination in research and I am especially grateful to my mentor Dr. Chris Law for his unwavering support of my research and professional goals.
