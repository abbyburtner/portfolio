Evolutionary biology research
  • My wonder about the natural world first drew me to research. Following that interest, I joined an evolutionary biology lab early in my first year of undergrad. One spring day at a lab meeting in 2021, we were discussing a postdoc’s project on the evolution of mammalian limbs when someone tangentially asked “how did bat limbs evolve to be so different from other mammals?”. I expressed my interest in this question and worked with my mentor to develop an independent research project on it. I collected the data for the project, ran (many) computational models, and wrote a first-authored manuscript. However, we couldn’t simply “throw in” the bats; the first six versions of the mathematical models didn’t work, so I had to readjust the methodology several times. Despite these challenges, as I worked on this independent project throughout my sophomore year—alongside three other research projects—I found myself more excited to spend time conducting research than on anything else. The hundreds of hours I spent on this project during the school year and summer convinced me that a path in research is the right fit for me. Read my manuscript here:
  • Burtner AE, Grossnickle DM, Santana SE, & Law CJ. In review. Gliding towards an understanding of the origin of flight in bats. In revision at PeerJ. Preprint at https://doi.org/10.1101/2022.09.26.509622.
